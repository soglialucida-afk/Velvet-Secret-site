Dreams and Symbols

Dreams rarely speak directly. They often show an image that feeling understands before reason does.

In dreams, something appears differently. Not as an explanation. Not as an instruction. As a scene that stays with us after waking.

We may dream in fragments. Water rising. A house with too many rooms. A door that will not open. A path that disappears before we reach the end. Such images rarely have one fixed meaning. Their power lies in the feeling they awaken.

That is why dreams should not be read like a dictionary where every word has a predetermined translation. A dream is not a sentence we simply translate into waking language. It is an inner scene. What happens matters. Who is present matters. What is missing matters. Above all, the feeling that remains after waking matters.

Dreams do not always tell us what a symbol means. They often show where a feeling has been hiding.

Carl Gustav Jung devoted much of his work to dreams and symbols. He did not treat dreams as meaningless random images, but as a way for the unconscious to approach conscious attention. This was not about predicting the future. It was about the relationship between the inner world and the images that appear within it.

A dream symbol is not necessarily a riddle that someone else must solve for us. A symbol can be a bridge. Something that connects a feeling, a memory, a fear, a desire or a question that has not yet found words during the day.

✦

Water can be peace. It can be a flood. It can be depth we are afraid to look into. It can be cleansing, grief or the feeling that something is carrying us. The meaning is not in water alone. Meaning forms between the water, the dream scene and the person who dreamed it.

A good way to begin is very simple. What was the main image? What was moving in the dream? What was still? What did you want to do but could not? Where was the feeling strongest?

Sometimes the answer is not in the most dramatic part of the dream. It may be in a small detail. In the colour of a room. In the fact that a door was open, but you did not enter. In the fact that you were searching for someone, but did not know who. Dreams often work exactly where the waking mind moves on too quickly.

Velvet Secret reads dreams as a symbolic space. Not as a diagnosis. Not as a prediction. Not as proof that something will happen. A dream is an invitation to listen. A gentle question: what is already moving in me, but has not yet found clear words?
